Ethical Considerations in Computing

An authoritative resource exploring the critical ethical issues surrounding machine learning, algorithmic fairness, and the pervasive integration of emerging technologies into daily life.

Part 1: Key Ideas in Machine Learning Ethics

Core Vulnerabilities

  • Accountability: Determining responsibility when AI fails (e.g., autonomous vehicle crashes).
  • Transparency: The "Black Box" problem where complex models cannot explain their logic.
  • Security & Privacy: Vulnerabilities to adversarial attacks and massive scraping of personal data without consent.

Algorithmic Bias & Data

  • "Garbage In, Garbage Out": Models inherit historical human biases present in their training data.
  • Amplification: AI automates prejudice at scale, cloaking biased decisions in mathematical "objectivity."
  • Fairness: The challenge of mathematically defining fairness across diverse demographics.

ML in Online Communication

Misinformation & Engagement

Recommendation algorithms often prioritize sensationalist or fake news because it maximizes user engagement and ad revenue.

Anonymity vs. Accountability

Balancing the right to free speech and privacy against the need to track bad actors utilizing deepfakes and botnets for harassment.

Part 2: Emerging Technologies in Daily Life

As technology escapes the screen and integrates into the physical world, the ethical stakes increase dramatically. This requires continual reassessment as the "Pacing Problem" ensures technology always outruns legislation.

QC Quantum Computing

Poses a massive threat to current data security infrastructure. The ability to break widely used encryption methods jeopardizes global financial stability and individual privacy.

XR Augmented & Virtual Reality (AR/VR)

Immersive environments amplify the psychological impact of actions (e.g., harassment). Headsets also enable hyper-surveillance, capturing intimate biometric data like eye tracking and pupil dilation.

AI Pervasive AI & Equity

Constant AI surveillance threatens civil liberties. Furthermore, a widening digital divide threatens those who cannot afford access to necessary computational resources, leaving them behind economically.
