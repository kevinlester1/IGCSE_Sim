What is Linear Regression?

Linear regression is a fundamental statistical method used to predict a continuous outcomeA numerical value that can take any value within a range (e.g., price, temperature, weight). based on one or more predictor variables.

The Core Idea

Imagine scattering points on a graph representing data. Linear regression tries to draw a "line of best fit" straight through the middle of those points. Once we have this line, we can use it to make predictions for new data points.

Example Use Case

Predicting the price of a house (continuous outcome) based on its size in square feet (predictor).

Another Example

Estimating a crop's yield (continuous outcome) based on the amount of rainfall (predictor).

The Variables

In simple linear regression, we deal with two types of variables. Understanding the relationship between them is the core of the model.

X Independent Variable

  • Also called the Predictor or Feature.
  • This is the variable we already know or control.
  • It is plotted on the horizontal (x-axis).
Example: Years of experience a person has.

Y Dependent Variable

  • Also called the Response or Target.
  • This is the continuous outcome we want to predict.
  • It is plotted on the vertical (y-axis).
Example: The salary they earn based on experience.

The Goal

Find a mathematical equation that accurately describes how changes in X lead to changes in Y.

The Equation of the Line

The "line of best fit" is defined by a simple algebraic equation that maps X to Y.

y = mSlope (weight/coefficient)x + bY-intercept (bias)

The linear regression model

m

The Slope (Coefficient)

The slope indicates the steepness and direction of the line.

Significance: It tells us how much the predicted outcome (y) changes on average for every one-unit increase in the predictor variable (x).
  • Positive m: As X increases, Y increases.
  • Negative m: As X increases, Y decreases.
b

The Y-Intercept

The intercept is the point where the line crosses the y-axis (where x = 0).

Significance: It represents the expected baseline value of the dependent variable (y) when the independent variable (x) is exactly zero.

Note: In some real-world contexts, x=0 might not make sense (e.g., predicting weight based on height), but the intercept mathematically anchors the line.

Assessing Model Fit: R²

Once we draw a line through our data, we need to know: How good is this line at predicting? Does the data tightly cluster around it, or is it scattered everywhere?

R-squared (R²)

The Coefficient of Determination

R² measures the proportion of variance in the dependent variable (Y) that can be explained by the independent variable (X) in our model.
0.0 (0%) 0.5 (50%) 1.0 (100%)
Poor Fit: Model explains almost nothing. Predictions are unreliable.
Moderate Fit: Model explains some variation.
Perfect Fit: Model explains all variation. Predictions match data perfectly.
Interpretation Example: If an R² value is 0.82 when predicting house price based on square footage, we can say that "82% of the variation in house prices is explained by the square footage of the house." The remaining 18% is due to other factors (location, age, etc.) not included in this simple model.

Adding More Dimensions

Real-world problems rarely depend on just one factor. Multiple Linear Regression extends our concept to include more than one independent variable (predictor).

From Lines to Planes

When we have two predictors (X₁ and X₂), our "line of best fit" becomes a "plane of best fit" in 3D space.

y = m₁x₁ + m₂x₂ + b

How to Read This

  • X-axis (Feature 1): e.g., House Size
  • Y-axis (Feature 2): e.g., Number of Bedrooms
  • Z-axis (Target): e.g., House Price
  • The blue points represent real data.
  • The translucent surface is our regression plane trying to pass as closely to all points as possible.
